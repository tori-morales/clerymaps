NYU prides itself on being a “campus without walls,” boasting an assortment of violet-flagged buildings scattered throughout Manhattan and parts of Brooklyn. For students, the lack of a traditional campus might be cause for grumbling about long walks between classes, but for the university, it poses a vital legal question — what counts as on-campus crime?


Since 1990, the federal government has required colleges and universities that receive government funds to publish annual statistics about certain categories of crime, including murder, aggravated assault and robbery. Institutions must also keep a daily log of all crimes reported to campus security officials.


Despite laying out the specific crimes that universities must report, the Jeanne Clery Act, which imposed the regulation, offers less guidance about the boundaries of the reporting area. This allows colleges across the country — urban and rural, public and private — to take their unique circumstances into account when developing reporting policies, but also means that university crime numbers depend on how reporting boundaries are drawn.


To understand the effect Clery Act regulation has on campus crime reporting, it's important to first understand the three property categories it uses.

On Campus

The Act defines on-campus crimes broadly: All crimes that happen in university buildings in or near the campus are considered to take place on campus. But the outer limits of the on-campus reporting area aren't clearly defined.


“Historically, there has not been a hard and fast answer with this to account for the fact that campuses are organized and set up rather differently depending on if they're in a more urban setting versus a rural setting,” said Laura Egan, senior program director at the Clery Center non-profit.


NYU's main campus, as the university calls it, includes 63 buildings surrounding Washington Square Park.


An additional 27 buildings nearby are counted on-campus for the purpose of crime reporting. All alleged crimes that take place in NYU buildings within the outer campus boundary are reported as on-campus crimes.

Public Property


The Clery Act's reporting requirements extend beyond an institution's buildings — but only slightly.


Colleges and universities must report crimes that happen on two types of public property. The first includes public property within the campus, and the second includes public property adjacent to and acessible from other on-campus buildings.


In most cases, adjacent and accessible public property refers to the sidewalk and street in front of a building, along with the sidewalk across the street, if one exists.



The sidewalk, street and opposite sidewalk in front of on-campus buildings must be included in universities' crime reports.
